# Signing in

You decide how people identify themselves when they contribute. There are three possibilities, covered in full on [[identity|Identity and accounts]]; this guide is about turning them on and choosing between them for your wiki.

## Sign in with GitHub

The quickest option when your contributors already use [[w:GitHub|GitHub]]. They click *Sign in with GitHub*, approve once, and from then on their edits carry their GitHub name and avatar. It's a single setting to enable.

## Sign in with a Wikigit account

For everyone who doesn't use GitHub. A Wikigit account is password-free: you enter an email, a one-time code arrives, you type it back, and you're in. It runs through a small sign-in service your wiki connects to — you can point at the one wikigit.org runs and be done, or host your own if you'd rather. Either way, the email stays with that service and never lands on a page or in the history.

## Anonymous editing

Your wiki can also let people edit without signing in at all, the way [[w:Wikipedia|Wikipedia]] does. [[anonymous-editing|Anonymous edits]] are credited to a short nickname. Leave it on for the widest front door and the occasional drive-by fix, or turn it off to require a name on every edit. A common middle ground is to allow anonymous edits but always [[editing-model|hold them for review]].

## Choosing

There's no single right answer; it depends on who you expect to contribute.

- A technical or open-source community → GitHub is probably enough on its own.
- A general audience → offer the email-based account so nobody is turned away for lacking GitHub.
- A wiki that wants the lowest possible barrier → leave anonymous editing on as well.

Whatever you pick, signing in is what lets a contributor build [[trust-and-moderation|standing]] over time, so their edits eventually publish without review.
