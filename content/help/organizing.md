# Organizing

A handful of pages organise themselves. A few hundred don't. None of what follows is needed to start — reach for it when the wiki gets big enough to need it.

## Page names and addresses

A page's name is its web address: "Getting started" lives at `/getting-started`. You can group related pages with a slash in the name — `help/editing` and `help/formatting` sit together under `help`, much like folders. (That's exactly how the pages in this Help section are arranged.)

## Categories

[[categories|Categories]] group pages by topic so readers can browse by subject. Add a page to one or more categories in [[reference/page-settings|its settings]] and it shows up as a chip at the foot of the page; clicking a category lists everything in it. Categories can be combined, nested inside broader ones, and kept separate from housekeeping labels like "draft" or "needs sources."

## The home page

Your front page is just another page, named `index`. Edit it like any other to set the tone — a welcome, a few key links, a sense of what the wiki is for.

## Renaming and moving

Renaming a page is a single action: pick the new name and the page moves, [[revisions|history]] and all. A gentle pointer is left at the old address so existing links still work — a **redirect**. You can also point one page at another on purpose, which is handy for alternate names of the same topic.

## Merging and splitting

As a wiki grows you sometimes need to reshape it:

- **Merge** folds one page into another and leaves a redirect behind, turning two overlapping pages into one.
- **Split** carves a section out of a long page into a page of its own.

Both keep the full history intact.

## Multiple languages

A page can exist in several languages as separate, linked pages, with a [[multilingual|language bar]] for readers and an offer to start any that's missing. Turn languages on in [[help/customize|Customize]].

## Finding things to fix

You don't have to hunt for loose ends by hand. Wikigit's [[special-pages|special pages]] surface them automatically — pages nothing links to, links to pages that don't exist yet, dead ends, and more. They make a useful to-do list as a wiki matures.
