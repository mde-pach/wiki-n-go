# Letting people sign in

Anyone can read and edit your wiki without an account — that's the default, and it keeps the barrier to helping out as low as possible. Signing in is **optional**, and it exists for one reason: so people who want credit for their work can have their name and picture on their edits.

## The three ways people contribute

1. **Anonymously.** No sign-in. Edits are credited to a short nickname. This is always available unless you turn it off.
2. **With GitHub.** People who have a GitHub account can sign in with it, and their edits show their GitHub name and avatar.
3. **With a Wikigit account.** For people who don't use GitHub, a Wikigit account is a simple, password-free sign-in: enter your email, get a one-time code, and you're in.

You decide which of these to offer. Many wikis are happy with just anonymous editing plus GitHub; offering Wikigit accounts is for when you expect contributors who aren't on GitHub.

## Why offer sign-in at all

- **Credit.** Regular contributors usually like having their work attributed to them.
- **Reputation.** Signed-in editors build up trust over time, so their edits can start going live without review (see [Managing changes](managing.md)).
- **Profiles.** Signed-in users can have a profile page and a list of their contributions.

## Privacy

Signing in never exposes private details on your wiki. Email addresses stay with the sign-in service and never appear on a page or in a page's history. Anonymous editors are identified only by a nickname — never by anything that could trace back to a real person.

## Turning it on

GitHub sign-in is a setting you switch on (see [Settings](settings.md)). Wikigit accounts need a small, separate sign-in service running — you can use a shared one or run your own, covered in the [Accounts service](accounts.md) guide.
